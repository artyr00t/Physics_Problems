## 5. Trigonometry

When a vector points at an angle, it is actually moving in two directions at once: **horizontally** (left/right) and **vertically** (up/down). We use trigonometry to find exactly how much of the vector's strength is going in each direction.

In this problem, we have:

* **Magnitude ($A$):** 15 (the total length of the "arrow")
* **Angle ($\theta$):** $60^\circ$ (the tilt from the ground)

### The Horizontal Component ($A_x$)

This is the part of the vector lying along the ground. We use the **Cosine** function because the horizontal side is "adjacent" (next to) the angle.

* Formula: $A_x = A \cdot \cos(\theta)$
* Calculation: $15 \cdot \cos(60^\circ)$
* Since $\cos(60^\circ) = 0.5$, we get: $15 \cdot 0.5 = \mathbf{7.5}$

### The Vertical Component ($A_y$)

This is the part of the vector pointing straight up. We use the **Sine** function because the vertical side is "opposite" the angle.

* Formula: $A_y = A \cdot \sin(\theta)$
* Calculation: $15 \cdot \sin(60^\circ)$
* Since $\sin(60^\circ) \approx 0.866$, we get: $15 \cdot 0.866 = \mathbf{12.99}$

**The Answer:** The vector is made of a horizontal push of **7.5** and a vertical push of approximately **12.99**.
