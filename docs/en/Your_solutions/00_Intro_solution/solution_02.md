## 2. Systems of Equations

Think of a **system of equations** as a puzzle where you have two different clues about two unknown numbers ($x$ and $y$). Your goal is to find the exact pair of numbers that makes **both** clues true at the same time.

We have these two clues:

1. **$2x + 3y = 12$**
2. **$x - y = 1$**

### Step 1: Isolate one variable

It is easiest to start with the second clue because it is simpler. Let's move the $y$ to the other side to see what $x$ equals:

* $x - y = 1$  $\rightarrow$  **$x = y + 1$**
* This tells us that $x$ is always exactly one unit larger than $y$.

### Step 2: Substitution (The "Swap")

Now, we go back to the first clue ($2x + 3y = 12$) and "swap" the $x$ with our new definition ($y + 1$):

* $2(y + 1) + 3y = 12$

### Step 3: Solve for $y$

Now we only have one letter ($y$) to deal with. We multiply the $2$ into the parentheses:

* $2y + 2 + 3y = 12$
* Combine the $y$ terms: $5y + 2 = 12$
* Subtract $2$ from both sides: $5y = 10$
* Divide by $5$: **$y = 2$**

### Step 4: Find $x$

Now that we know $y$ is $2$, we go back to our simplest clue ($x = y + 1$):

* $x = 2 + 1$
* **$x = 3$**

**The Answer:** The only point where these two clues meet is at **$x = 3, y = 2$**. If you plug these into the original equations, both will work perfectly ($2(3) + 3(2) = 12$ and $3 - 2 = 1).$
