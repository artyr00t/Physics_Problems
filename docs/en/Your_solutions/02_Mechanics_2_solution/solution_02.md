**2. Harmonic Motion**

**A 10 kg mass is attached to a spring and oscillates according to the equation $x(t) = 0.2 \cos(10\pi t)$ (in meters). What is the spring constant $k$? What is the total mechanical energy of the system?**

---

**Step 1: Reading the "Bouncing" Recipe**
When you see an equation like $x(t) = 0.2 \cos(10\pi t)$, it is actually a secret recipe that tells you two very important things about the spring:
* **Amplitude ($A$):** The number in the front ($0.2$) tells you the spring stretches exactly **$0.2$ meters** away from the center.
* **Angular Frequency ($\omega$):** The number stuck to the time ($10\pi$) tells you how fast the mass is "spinning" through its bounce. So, $\omega = 10\pi$.



**Step 2: Finding the Spring Constant ($k$)**
The "Spring Constant" is just a fancy way of saying "How stiff is this spring?" To find it, we use the mass ($10 \text{ kg}$) and our frequency ($10\pi$). 
The rule is: $\omega^2 = \frac{k}{m}$ (The bounce speed depends on stiffness and weight).

1.  **Rearrange the formula:** $k = m \cdot \omega^2$
2.  **Plug in the numbers:** $k = 10 \cdot (10\pi)^2$
3.  **Square the frequency:** $(10\pi) \times (10\pi) = 100\pi^2$
4.  **Final Calculation:** $k = 10 \times 100\pi^2 = \mathbf{1000\pi^2 \text{ N/m}}$
*(If you use $\pi^2 \approx 9.87$, then $k \approx \mathbf{9870 \text{ N/m}}$).*

**Step 3: Finding the Total Energy ($E$)**
Energy in a spring is like a battery. It's at its maximum when the spring is stretched all the way out. At that point, all the energy is "Spring Energy" (Potential Energy). 

The formula for Total Energy is: $E = \frac{1}{2} k A^2$

1.  **Use our $k$ from before:** $1000\pi^2$
2.  **Use our stretch distance ($A$):** $0.2 \text{ meters}$
3.  **Plug them in:** $E = 0.5 \cdot (1000\pi^2) \cdot (0.2)^2$
4.  **Square the stretch:** $0.2 \times 0.2 = 0.04$
5.  **Final Calculation:** $E = 500\pi^2 \cdot 0.04 = \mathbf{20\pi^2 \text{ Joules}}$
*(If you use $\pi^2 \approx 9.87$, then $E \approx \mathbf{197.4 \text{ Joules}}$).*



**Conclusion:**
* **Spring Constant ($k$):** The spring is very stiff, roughly **$9870 \text{ Newtons}$** of force are needed to stretch it one meter.
* **Total Energy ($E$):** The system has about **$197$ Joules** of energy total, which just keeps swapping between "speed" and "stretching" as it bounces.
