**3. Conservation of Energy**

**A pendulum with a length of 1.0 meter is released from an initial angle of 15°. What is the speed of the pendulum bob at the bottom of its swing?**

---

**Step 1: The Energy Swap (Potential to Kinetic)**
Think of a pendulum like a slide. At the very top (15°), the ball is sitting still but it is "high up." It has **Potential Energy** ($PE$). When you let go, it falls and turns all that height into **Speed** (**Kinetic Energy**, $KE$) at the bottom.
* **Top:** All Potential Energy ($mgh$)
* **Bottom:** All Kinetic Energy ($\frac{1}{2}mv^2$)

Because energy is never lost, we say: $mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$.
If we cancel the mass ($m$), the formula for speed is: $v = \sqrt{2gh}$



**Step 2: Finding the Height ($h$)**
To find the speed, we first need to know exactly how many meters the ball "dropped." We use a little bit of geometry for this.
* The string is $1.0$ meter long ($L$).
* When it's at an angle of 15°, the vertical distance from the top is $L \cdot \cos(15^\circ)$.
* The "drop" ($h$) is the total length minus that vertical distance.

**Calculation:**
* $\cos(15^\circ) \approx 0.966$
* Vertical distance = $1.0 \times 0.966 = 0.966 \text{ meters}$
* **Height ($h$):** $1.0 - 0.966 = \mathbf{0.034 \text{ meters}}$



**Step 3: Calculating the Final Speed ($v$)**
Now we just plug our height ($0.034\text{ m}$) and gravity ($9.8\text{ m/s}^2$) into our speed formula.

**Calculation:**
* $v = \sqrt{2 \cdot 9.8 \cdot 0.034}$
* $v = \sqrt{19.6 \cdot 0.034}$
* $v = \sqrt{0.6664}$
* **Result:** $v \approx \mathbf{0.816 \text{ m/s}}$

**Conclusion:**
At the very bottom of the swing, the pendulum is moving at about **$0.82 \text{ meters per second}$**. Even though it started at a 15° angle, it only dropped about 3 centimeters, which is why it isn't moving very fast!
